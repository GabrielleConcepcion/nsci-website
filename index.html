What is Information Society

The term information society is closely associated with the field of information and communications technology (ICT). In common usage, an information society can be simply understood as a society where the usage and knowledge of information and computer technology is at a high level.

The concept of the Information Society emerged in the mid-20th century, with roots in earlier technological revolutions. The idea gained prominence in the 1960s and 1970s, influenced by the work of economists like Fritz Machlup, who analyzed the growing importance of knowledge production and distribution.

Human Quest for Understanding

In the human quest for understanding the natural world, the ability to name and classify object found in nature was seen as a first step in knowing. Thus, the scientific search for truth early on recognized the usefulness of language and the ability it gave to make sense of nature. For the ancient Greeks, language was an object worthy of admiration. Words have power. This kind of knowing sprang from the Greek fascination and wonder at the power of words and language

Power of Words

Science, from the Latin word scire (meaning to know), is one kind of knowledge the Greeks wanted to understand. The idea of comprehending words as more than just combinations of sounds led the Greeks to seek out the principles of everyday language. When talking to other people, for example, a meaningful message is created using ordinary sounds. Its meaning is also not diminished by multiplication - the speaker can use the same words over and over again to talk to ten, a hundred, or even a thousand people separately or at the same time.

Common Thread

Nevertheless, the same message will be received by everyone. Words, therefore, can function across space and time without reducing their meaning. The first philosophers, as they thrashed about groping for and seeking a unifying principle in nature, sometimes hit upon things such as fire or water. But they believed that something was common in all of these. The many seemingly different things in the natural world must have a unifying factor. There was an inside to be "understood." They sought for this meta phusis, literally meaning "after nature" (De Chardin, 1965). Plato's principle of "One and the Many" refers to the underlying unity among diverse beings in the natural world. For Plato, there is a common intrinsic nature shared by different objects, which determines their real sense. Biologists devised a way to illustrate this principle using a system differentiating between genus and species. Many species belong in one genus. In the 21st century, we are aware more than ever that there is rich diversity in nature, which technology has allowed us to discover (BANWA Natural Science, 2008).

Mathematics in shaping Modern Knowledge Systems

Language acts as the primary medium for expressing, transmitting, and structuring knowledge. It shapes perception through linguistic relativity (Sapir-Whorf hypothesis), where language structures influence cognitive processes like spatial awareness and time perception. For instance, the Kuuk Thaayorre people’s directional language redefines their spatial cognition.

Mathematics provides a universal framework for modeling phenomena, driving scientific and technological progress. It underpins innovations from physics (Newton’s laws) to AI algorithms. During the Industrial Revolution, mathematical paradigms in measurement and calculation-not just scientific theory-enabled advancements in engineering, economics, and cartography. Modern data-driven fields rely on mathematics for optimizing systems, predicting trends, and cryptography Ethical and technical challenges in a connected, digital world.

Both language and mathematics are symbolic systems that help us represent, communicate, and process complex ideas. Language uses words, grammar, and syntax to convey meaning, while mathematics uses symbols, numbers, and formulas. Both systems rely on rules and structures to create and share knowledge

Historical milestones in Information Society

Printing Press

Invented by Johannes Gutenberg around 1440, the printing press introduced movable type, allowing for the rapid, cost-effective mass production of books and documents. Before this, texts were laboriously hand-copied, limiting access to knowledge to elites and religious institutions. The printing press made literature, scientific works, and educational materials widely available, leading to increased literacy rates and the spread of new ideas. It played a pivotal role in major intellectual movements such as the Renaissance, Reformation, and Scientific Revolution by enabling thinkers to disseminate their work to a much broader audience. This technology laid the foundation for modern publishing and mass media, fostering public discourse and informed societies. Its legacy is seen today in the continued importance of printed materials and the principles of mass communication

World Wide Web

The World Wide Web (WWW), invented by Tim Berners-Lee in 1989 at CERN, revolutionized the way information is accessed and shared globally. Berners-Lee developed the Web as a system of hypertext documents linked across the Internet, enabling users to navigate easily between resources via browsers and servers. By 1991, the Web was publicly available, and its protocols, including HTTP and HTML, were made royalty-free in 1993, which accelerated its adoption worldwide. The rise of the Internet, initially a military and academic network, combined with the Web’s user-friendly interface, transformed global communication and information exchange. The 1990s saw rapid growth in personal computer ownership and Internet accessibility, leading to mass commercialization and widespread use for email, information sharing, and e-commerce. The Web enabled diverse content and services, making it a central platform in everyday life and fostering the development of the information society. This transformation profoundly affected information access by democratizing knowledge, breaking down geographic and institutional barriers, and enabling real-time global communication.
